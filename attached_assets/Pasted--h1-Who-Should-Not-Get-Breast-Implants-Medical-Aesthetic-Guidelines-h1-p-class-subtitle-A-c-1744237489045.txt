<section id="section-1">
  <h2>Medical Conditions That May Exclude Candidates</h2>
  <p>There are certain medical conditions and physiological factors that may disqualify someone from safely undergoing breast augmentation surgery. These include:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Autoimmune diseases</strong> (e.g. lupus, scleroderma): Increased risk of adverse response to implant materials.</li>
    <li><strong>Uncontrolled diabetes or hypertension</strong>: Increases risk of post-op complications and poor wound healing.</li>
    <li><strong>History of blood clotting disorders</strong>: Increases risk of DVT/PE post-operatively.</li>
    <li><strong>Severe allergies or anaphylaxis history</strong>: May complicate anesthesia or material compatibility.</li>
    <li><strong>Active infections</strong> (skin or systemic): Implants should never be placed during ongoing infection.</li>
    <li><strong>Current pregnancy or breastfeeding</strong>: Surgery should be deferred until 3–6 months postpartum and post-lactation.</li>
  </ul>
</section>

<section id="section-2">
  <h2>Psychological Considerations & Age Requirements</h2>
  <p>Breast augmentation has emotional and psychological implications. Surgeons screen for:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Body dysmorphic disorder (BDD)</strong>: Unrealistic expectations or dissatisfaction regardless of outcome.</li>
    <li><strong>External pressure</strong> from partners or social media trends.</li>
    <li><strong>Unrealistic goals</strong>: e.g., wanting to look like a specific celebrity or boost self-worth entirely through physical changes.</li>
  </ul>
  <p><strong>Age limitations:</strong> In the U.S., the FDA mandates:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Minimum age for saline implants: <strong>18 years</strong></li>
    <li>Minimum age for silicone implants: <strong>22 years</strong></li>
  </ul>
  <p>Teenagers are generally not considered candidates unless under rare reconstructive exceptions (e.g. congenital asymmetry, trauma).</p>
</section>

<section id="faq">
  <h2>Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>Can I get implants if I have an autoimmune disease?</h4>
    <p>Some surgeons may proceed with caution depending on the severity and control of the disease, but many avoid elective implants due to immune activation risk.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>Is smoking a disqualification?</h4>
    <p>Active smokers are typically told to quit for 4–6 weeks before and after surgery. Smoking impairs healing and increases the risk of necrosis and infection.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>Can I still get implants after pregnancy?</h4>
    <p>Yes, but most surgeons recommend waiting at least 3–6 months after delivery and lactation to allow tissue to stabilize.</p>
  </div>
  <div class="faq-item">
    <h4>What if I have a history of depression?</h4>
    <p>Mild depression does not disqualify you, but active severe depression or unmanaged mental health conditions may require clearance from a psychiatrist.</p>
  </div>
</section>
