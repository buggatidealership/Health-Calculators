What does my vitamin D level mean?

Frequently Asked Questions

  • The conversion factor is 2.496 (often rounded to 2.5). To convert ng/mL to nmol/L, multiply by 2.496. To convert nmol/L to ng/mL, divide by 2.496 (or multiply by 0.4). This factor is derived from the molecular weight of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (calcidiol) at 400.65 g/mol.

  • Most US labs report vitamin D in ng/mL, while laboratories in Europe, Canada, and Australia typically use nmol/L. The unit should be listed on your lab report next to your 25(OH)D result. If your number is in the single digits to ~100, it is likely ng/mL. If it is in the range of 25–250, it could be either — check the label.

  • Levels below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) are considered deficient. Levels of 20–29 ng/mL (50–72 nmol/L) are insufficient. Levels of 30–100 ng/mL (75–250 nmol/L) are sufficient. Above 100 ng/mL (250 nmol/L) is considered potentially toxic. Note: some practitioners and researchers consider 40–60 ng/mL (100–150 nmol/L) the optimal range, though this is debated.

  • The US has traditionally used weight-based measurements (ng/mL), while many other countries adopted the International System of Units (SI), which uses substance concentration (nmol/L). Both measure the same thing — your blood level of 25-hydroxyvitamin D. Neither is more accurate; they are simply different scales.

  • Vitamin D testing measures the level of 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] in your blood, which is the primary circulating form and the best indicator of your vitamin D status. The test requires a simple blood draw and results are typically available within a few days. Methods include immunoassay and LC-MS/MS, which may produce slightly different values.

Methodology

This calculator converts between the two standard units used to report serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] levels: ng/mL (nanograms per milliliter, conventional units used primarily in the United States) and nmol/L (nanomoles per liter, SI units used internationally).

nmol/L = ng/mL × 2.496

The conversion factor of 2.496 is derived from the molecular weight of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (calcidiol) at 400.65 g/mol. In clinical practice, the rounded factor of 2.5 is commonly used: ng/mL × 2.5 = nmol/L and nmol/L × 0.4 = ng/mL.

Reference ranges for vitamin D status classification (deficient, insufficient, sufficient, potentially toxic) are based on the Endocrine Society 2011 clinical practice guideline and the 2024 update. These thresholds are population-level guidelines; individual optimal levels may vary based on clinical context. Laboratory assays for 25(OH)D can vary by method (immunoassay vs. LC-MS/MS), so minor discrepancies between labs are expected.

To convert vitamin D levels: multiply ng/mL by 2.496 to get nmol/L, or divide nmol/L by 2.496 to get ng/mL. US labs typically report in ng/mL, while UK, European, and Australian labs use nmol/L. A level below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) is considered deficient. Levels of 20-29 ng/mL (50-72 nmol/L) are insufficient. The sufficient range is 30-100 ng/mL (75-250 nmol/L). Above 100 ng/mL (250 nmol/L) is potentially toxic. The Endocrine Society recommends maintaining levels of at least 30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L) for bone health, though some researchers suggest 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L) as optimal.
