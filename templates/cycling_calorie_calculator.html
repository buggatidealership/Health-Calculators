How Many Calories Does Cycling Burn?

Cycling burns roughly 250–1,000 calories per hour, depending on your body weight and cycling intensity. A 155 lb (70 kg) person cycling at a moderate pace (12–14 mph) burns approximately 560 kcal/hour. Casual leisurely cycling burns around 280 kcal/hour, while racing pace (16–19 mph) can burn over 840 kcal/hour for the same person. Stationary bike at moderate effort burns around 385 kcal/hour — less than outdoor cycling at the same perceived intensity due to the absence of wind resistance.

How Cycling Intensity Affects Calorie Burn

Cycling at 14–16 mph burns roughly 2.5 times more calories per hour than casual <10 mph riding. The MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) for cycling ranges from 4.0 (leisurely) to 15.8 (elite race pace). Because calories = MET × body weight × hours, doubling your speed from 10 mph to 20 mph more than doubles your calorie burn — speed has a non-linear effect on energy expenditure because aerodynamic drag increases with the square of velocity.

The key factors driving cycling calorie burn are:

Primary Factors Affecting Cycling Calorie Burn

  • Speed / intensity: The single biggest factor. Racing pace (16–19 mph) burns 3× more than leisurely (<10 mph). Air resistance increases as the square of speed — above 15 mph, overcoming wind drag dominates energy expenditure.
  • Body weight: Heavier riders burn more calories at every intensity because more mass requires more energy to propel. A 205 lb rider burns ~33% more than a 155 lb rider at the same speed.
  • Duration: Calories scale linearly with time at a constant intensity — 60 minutes burns exactly twice as many calories as 30 minutes.
  • Terrain: Hills dramatically increase calorie burn — climbing requires significant additional energy to overcome gravity. This calculator uses general/flat MET values; actual burn on hilly terrain will be higher.
  • Bike type and fit: Aerodynamic road bikes are more efficient than mountain bikes for the same speed. A rider at 12 mph on a mountain bike works harder than on a road bike.

A 2013 study in the Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport (Peveler et al.) found that air resistance accounts for over 90% of total resistance at speeds above 15 mph on flat terrain — explaining why small increases in speed above 15 mph require exponentially more power and calorie expenditure.

Key Difference: Perceived Effort vs. Actual Calorie Burn

  • On a stationary bike, "moderate" effort typically corresponds to MET 5.5 — similar to road cycling at 10–12 mph.
  • Peloton/spin bikes with resistance can reach MET 8.5–10+ at high effort, matching vigorous outdoor cycling.
  • Recumbent bikes generally burn slightly fewer calories than upright bikes at the same resistance because the reclined position reduces core muscle engagement.
  • Heart rate monitors combined with power meters give the most accurate individual calorie estimates for cycling.

Methodology: MET-Based Calorie Formula

This calculator uses the MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) method, with values from the 2011 Compendium of Physical Activities (Ainsworth et al., Med Sci Sports Exerc, 2011) — the most widely cited reference for exercise energy expenditure in exercise science research.

Core Formula:

Calories (kcal) = MET × weight (kg) × duration (hours)

One MET equals 3.5 mL O₂/kg/min — the metabolic rate at rest. An activity with MET 8.0 consumes 8× more oxygen (and energy) than resting. Multiplying by body weight accounts for the fact that moving more mass requires more energy. Multiplying by hours converts rate to total expenditure.

MET Values Used (from the 2011 Compendium, code 01009–01060):

  • Road cycling <10 mph: 4.0
  • Road cycling 10–11.9 mph: 6.8
  • Road cycling 12–13.9 mph: 8.0
  • Road cycling 14–15.9 mph: 10.0
  • Road cycling 16–19 mph: 12.0
  • Road cycling ≥20 mph: 15.8
  • Mountain biking: 8.5
  • Stationary bike — light: 3.5 / moderate: 5.5 / vigorous: 8.5

Fat Burned Estimate: Fat oxidation fraction varies with exercise intensity. At low intensity (MET <4), approximately 60% of calories come from fat. At moderate intensity (MET 4–6), ~50% fat. At vigorous intensity (MET 6–9), ~40% fat. At high intensity (MET ≥9), ~25% fat — the body increasingly relies on carbohydrates (Achten & Jeukendrup, 2004). Fat energy density used: 9.44 kcal/g (Frayn, 1983).

Limitations: The MET method provides population-average estimates. Individual variation of ±15–20% is normal depending on fitness level, body composition, cycling efficiency, equipment, terrain, and wind conditions. The Compendium MET values are assigned to speed ranges and do not account for wind, hills, drafting, or equipment differences within a speed category. Heart rate monitors with power meters provide more individualized estimates for serious cyclists.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories does cycling burn per hour?

Cycling burns approximately 280–840 calories per hour for a 155 lb (70 kg) person, depending on intensity. Leisurely riding (<10 mph) burns about 280 kcal/hour. Moderate cycling (12–14 mph) burns roughly 560 kcal/hour. Racing pace (16–19 mph) burns 840+ kcal/hour. Body weight is also significant — a 205 lb person burns ~33% more than a 155 lb person at the same intensity.

Does cycling burn belly fat?

Cycling burns overall body fat — including abdominal fat — when combined with a calorie deficit. You cannot spot-reduce fat from specific areas, but research confirms that regular aerobic exercise like cycling reduces visceral (abdominal) fat. A 2012 study in the Journal of Obesity found that moderate-intensity cycling 3x/week significantly reduced total body fat and waist circumference over 12 weeks, even without dietary changes.

How accurate are cycling calorie counters on bikes and apps?

Stationary bike calorie displays typically overestimate by 15–30% using simplified formulas that ignore individual weight and fitness level. GPS apps using power meter data are the most accurate. Apps estimating from GPS speed alone have 10–20% error. This calculator uses validated MET values from the 2011 Compendium of Physical Activities, providing population-average estimates with ~±15% individual variation.

Is cycling or running better for burning calories?

Running generally burns more calories per hour than cycling at the same perceived effort because it engages more muscle mass. Running at 6 mph (MET ~10) burns about 700 kcal/hour for a 155 lb person — similar to vigorous cycling at 14–16 mph. However, cycling is lower impact and allows longer sessions with less injury risk, potentially making total weekly calorie burn higher for many people. For equivalent calorie burn, cycling requires approximately 20–25% longer sessions than running.

How many calories does a 30-minute bike ride burn?

A 30-minute bike ride burns approximately 140–420 calories for a 155 lb (70 kg) person depending on intensity: leisurely <10 mph (~140 kcal), easy 10–12 mph (~238 kcal), moderate 12–14 mph (~280 kcal), vigorous 14–16 mph (~350 kcal), racing 16–19 mph (~420 kcal). Use the calculator above for your personal estimate.

How many calories does mountain biking burn vs. road cycling?

Mountain biking has a MET of 8.5, equivalent to road cycling at 12–14 mph (MET 8.0) in calorie terms. A 155 lb person burns ~595 kcal/hour mountain biking vs. ~560 kcal/hour at moderate road cycling pace. Mountain biking burns slightly more due to technical terrain, a heavier bike, and greater core and upper body engagement. Highly technical off-trail riding can push actual MET values above the Compendium average of 8.5.
