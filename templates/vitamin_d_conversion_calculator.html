How Do Vitamin D Units Convert?

Vitamin D blood levels are measured in ng/mL (conventional, used in the US) or nmol/L (SI units, used internationally). The conversion factor is 2.496, based on the molecular weight of 25-hydroxyvitamin D (calcidiol) at 400.65 g/mol. About Vitamin D Units

  • Conversion rate: 1 ng/mL = 2.5 nmol/L
  • Blood tests: US typically uses ng/mL, while many international countries use nmol/L
  • Normal Range: Generally 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L) is considered adequate
  • Deficiency: Below 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) is typically considered deficient
  • Important note: Always follow healthcare provider recommendations for your specific needs

Why Converting Vitamin D Units Matters

Different countries use different measurement systems for vitamin D:

  • United States: Typically uses ng/mL (nanograms per milliliter)
  • Europe, Canada, Australia: Usually use nmol/L (nanomoles per liter)

Converting between these units helps you compare your results with international guidelines and research.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the conversion factor between ng/mL and nmol/L?

The conversion factor is 2.5. To convert:

  • ng/mL to nmol/L: Multiply by 2.5
  • nmol/L to ng/mL: Divide by 2.5 (or multiply by 0.4)

Which unit does my lab use?

Most US labs report vitamin D in ng/mL, while laboratories in Europe, Canada, and Australia typically use nmol/L. The unit should be listed on your lab report.

What is a healthy vitamin D level?

Generally, vitamin D levels are categorized as:

  • Deficient: Less than 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L)
  • Insufficient: 21-29 ng/mL (51-74 nmol/L)
  • Sufficient: 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L)
  • High: Above 50 ng/mL (125 nmol/L)

These ranges may vary depending on the laboratory and medical guidelines. Always consult with your healthcare provider about your specific results.

Why do different countries use different units?

The use of different units stems from historical preferences in measurement systems. The US has traditionally favored weight-based measurements (ng/mL), while many other countries have adopted the International System of Units (SI), which uses substance concentration (nmol/L).

How does vitamin D testing work?

Vitamin D testing typically measures the level of 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] in your blood, which is the primary circulating form of vitamin D and the best indicator of your vitamin D status. The test requires a simple blood sample and can be ordered by your healthcare provider.
