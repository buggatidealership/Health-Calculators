What Is Zone 2 Heart Rate Training?

Zone 2 heart rate training refers to exercising at 60–70% of your maximum heart rate — an intensity where you can hold a full conversation but still feel a clear aerobic effort. At this pace, your body primarily burns fat for fuel, recruits slow-twitch muscle fibers, and stimulates the production of new mitochondria (the energy factories inside your cells). Zone 2 is the foundation of aerobic fitness and is used by elite endurance athletes and longevity-focused individuals alike.

Zone 2 gained mainstream attention through the work of Dr. Inigo San Millán, a sports physiologist who studied Tour de France cyclists, and Dr. Peter Attia, who popularized its role in longevity. The core finding: most people train too hard too often — spending time in Zone 3 ("grey zone" or "junk miles") that is too easy to drive adaptation but too hard to recover from quickly. Zone 2 delivers superior aerobic adaptation per unit of fatigue.

Why Zone 2 Training Is So Effective

Zone 2 training sits at the sweet spot where aerobic adaptation is maximized and recovery demand is minimized. Here's what happens physiologically when you train consistently in Zone 2:

Mitochondrial Biogenesis

Zone 2 exercise is the most powerful stimulus for mitochondrial biogenesis — the creation of new mitochondria in muscle cells. Mitochondria are the organelles that convert fat and glucose into ATP (energy). More mitochondria means more metabolic capacity. Research by San Millán and Brooks (2018) found that elite cyclists had 2–3× more mitochondria per muscle fiber than untrained individuals, almost entirely from Zone 2 training.

Fat Oxidation Efficiency

At Zone 2 intensity, roughly 55–70% of energy comes from fat (versus glucose). Training in this zone repeatedly upregulates fat oxidation enzymes — particularly those in the electron transport chain and beta-oxidation pathways. The result: your body becomes more efficient at burning fat at rest and during exercise, improving body composition and metabolic health. This is distinct from "fat-burning zone" myths — Zone 2 doesn't burn more fat per minute than higher intensities, but it builds the machinery to burn more fat overall.

Lactate Dynamics

Zone 2 is defined by some researchers as the highest intensity at which lactate is still being cleared as fast as it's produced — around 2 mmol/L blood lactate. Below this threshold, you're fully aerobic. Above it, lactate accumulates and you shift from Zone 2 to Zone 3. Training in Zone 2 improves the lactate shuttle — the mechanism by which slow-twitch muscles clear lactate produced by fast-twitch fibers — directly improving endurance performance.

How to Train in Zone 2 Effectively

Recommended Weekly Volume

Dr. Inigo San Millán recommends 3–4 hours of Zone 2 per week, split into sessions of 45–90 minutes each. The minimum effective dose is thought to be around 2–3 hours/week. Elite endurance athletes often spend 6–10 hours/week in Zone 2.

The 80/20 Training Rule

Research on elite endurance athletes consistently shows they spend approximately 80% of training time at or below Zone 2 and only 20% at higher intensities (Zone 4–5). This polarized training distribution produces better results than spending time in Zone 3 (moderate intensity), which provides less adaptation per unit of fatigue.

The Talk Test

The gold standard non-tech check: you should be able to speak in complete sentences, but a long monologue would feel slightly labored. If you can't get full sentences out, slow down. If you could sing comfortably, speed up. The talk test correlates to approximately 60–70% of VO₂ max — the true Zone 2.

Frequently Asked Questions

What exactly is Zone 2 heart rate?

Zone 2 heart rate is the intensity level — typically 60–70% of your maximum heart rate — at which your body is working aerobically using a mix of fat and glucose for fuel, primarily fat. It is the highest intensity where you can still hold a full conversation, corresponding to approximately 2 mmol/L blood lactate in lab testing. Zone 2 is the primary training zone used to build aerobic base, improve fat oxidation, and stimulate mitochondrial biogenesis.

Why does Zone 2 training build mitochondria?

Zone 2 training activates PGC-1α (peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor gamma coactivator 1-alpha), the master regulator of mitochondrial biogenesis. This signaling cascade stimulates the growth of new mitochondria in slow-twitch (Type I) muscle fibers over weeks of consistent training. Higher mitochondrial density means more energy can be produced aerobically — improving both endurance performance and metabolic health. Research shows that consistent Zone 2 training over 4–8 weeks significantly increases mitochondrial enzyme activity (Holloszy & Coyle, 1984; San Millán & Brooks, 2018).

How accurate is the 220 − age max HR formula?

The "220 − age" formula is a rough estimate with a standard deviation of about ±12 bpm — meaning for a 40-year-old with an estimated max HR of 180 bpm, their true max HR could reasonably be anywhere from 156–204 bpm. The Tanaka formula (208 − 0.7 × age) used in this calculator is more accurate for adults, especially those over 40, based on a meta-analysis of 351 studies (Tanaka et al., 2001). The most accurate way to determine max HR is a maximal effort test (e.g., timed mile run, VO₂ max test) or referencing the highest HR recorded during intense exercise.

What is the Maffetone MAF Method?

The Maffetone MAF (Maximum Aerobic Function) method was developed by coach Phil Maffetone and used with athletes like Mark Allen (6× Ironman World Champion). The formula is 180 − age, with adjustments of ±5–10 based on training history and health status. The idea is that this ceiling keeps athletes fully in aerobic metabolism. The Maffetone zone tends to be more conservative than % Max HR calculations — many runners find they need to slow to a jog or even walk to stay at MAF. Despite feeling easy, training consistently at MAF for 3–6 months typically produces significant aerobic gains.

How much Zone 2 training should I do per week?

Research and elite coaching suggest 3–4 hours per week as the optimal dose for meaningful mitochondrial adaptation — done in sessions of 45–90 minutes. Sessions shorter than 30 minutes provide little Zone 2 benefit. The 80/20 principle (80% of weekly training volume in Zone 1–2, 20% in Zone 4–5) is well-supported for endurance development. If you're new to structured training, start with 2–3 sessions per week of 30–45 minutes and build up over 8–12 weeks.

Why does Zone 2 sometimes feel too easy?

For beginners and moderately active people, Zone 2 often feels surprisingly slow — you may need to run at a pace that feels like jogging or even walking briskly to keep your heart rate in the zone. This is normal and reflects how most recreational exercisers chronically train too hard. Ironically, the "easy" pace is what drives the most sustainable aerobic adaptation. Over 3–6 months of consistent Zone 2 training, your pace at the same heart rate will naturally increase as your aerobic base improves — a sign that the training is working.

Can I use a fitness watch to monitor Zone 2?

Yes — modern fitness watches (Garmin, Apple Watch, Polar, Whoop) can track heart rate continuously during exercise. Use the custom heart rate zones feature and enter your calculated Zone 2 range. Optical HR sensors on wrists are accurate to within ±5 bpm during steady-state cardio; they're less accurate during high-intensity intervals or activities with wrist movement like rowing. A chest strap (Garmin HRM, Polar H10) is the gold standard for accuracy.

Methodology

This calculator implements three established methods for determining Zone 2 heart rate training range:

Percentage of Max HR: Zone 2 = 60–70% of maximum heart rate. When max HR is not provided, it is estimated using the Tanaka formula: Max HR = 208 − (0.7 × age). The Tanaka formula is based on a meta-analysis of 351 published studies totaling 18,712 subjects (Tanaka et al., 2001) and outperforms the traditional "220 − age" formula, particularly for adults over 40.

Karvonen Heart Rate Reserve (HRR) Method: Zone 2 = (60–70% × HRR) + Resting HR, where HRR = Max HR − Resting HR. This method was developed by Finnish physiologist Martti Karvonen and published in 1957. It accounts for individual fitness level — a fit person with a lower resting HR will have a higher Zone 2 ceiling in absolute bpm than an unfit person with the same max HR.

Maffetone MAF Method: Maximum Aerobic Function HR = 180 − age ± adjustment for training history. The ±5 and ±10 adjustments are from Maffetone's clinical protocol. This method defines an upper heart rate ceiling for aerobic training rather than a traditional zone range. The range used is MAF−10 to MAF. Note that MAF calculations typically yield lower absolute values than % Max HR methods, reflecting Maffetone's conservative approach to keeping athletes fully aerobic.
