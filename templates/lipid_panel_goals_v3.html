Frequently Asked Questions

  • A normal lipid panel shows total cholesterol below 200 mg/dL, LDL below 100 mg/dL (optimal), HDL above 40 mg/dL for men or 50 mg/dL for women, and triglycerides below 150 mg/dL. Non-HDL cholesterol (total minus HDL) should ideally be below 130 mg/dL.

  • LDL (low-density lipoprotein) is "bad" cholesterol — high levels cause plaque buildup in arteries. HDL (high-density lipoprotein) is "good" cholesterol — it removes LDL from your bloodstream. You want LDL low and HDL high. Think of LDL as delivery trucks dumping cholesterol in your arteries, and HDL as cleanup trucks hauling it away.

  • Non-HDL cholesterol equals total cholesterol minus HDL. It captures all atherogenic (artery-clogging) particles including LDL, VLDL, and Lp(a). Many cardiologists consider it a better single predictor of cardiovascular risk than LDL alone, especially when triglycerides are elevated above 200 mg/dL.

  • A TC:HDL ratio below 3.5 is optimal. Between 3.5 and 5.0 is acceptable. Above 5.0 indicates elevated cardiovascular risk. The ratio matters because having high total cholesterol with very high HDL is less concerning than the same total with low HDL.

  • Yes. Regular exercise (150+ minutes per week), eating more fiber and vegetables, limiting saturated fat, losing excess weight, and quitting smoking can all improve your lipid panel. However, some people need statins due to genetics (familial hypercholesterolemia) or very high LDL. Discuss options with your doctor.

  • Adults with normal risk should get a lipid panel every 4-6 years. If you have risk factors for heart disease, abnormal previous results, or are on cholesterol medication, your doctor may recommend annual testing or more frequently.

  • Traditional guidance recommends 9-12 hours of fasting before a lipid panel. However, recent research shows non-fasting lipid panels are acceptable for initial screening. Fasting primarily affects triglyceride levels. If your non-fasting triglycerides are above 200 mg/dL, your doctor may order a fasting retest.

Methodology

This calculator interprets lipid panel results using clinical thresholds from the 2018 AHA/ACC Multisociety Guideline on Blood Cholesterol Management and the NCEP ATP III guidelines.

Total cholesterol is classified as desirable (< 200), borderline high (200-239), or high (≥ 240). LDL uses a five-tier scale: optimal (< 100), near optimal (100-129), borderline high (130-159), high (160-189), and very high (≥ 190). HDL thresholds are sex-specific: low is < 40 mg/dL for males and < 50 mg/dL for females; ≥ 60 mg/dL is considered protective. Triglycerides are normal (< 150), borderline high (150-199), high (200-499), or very high (≥ 500).

Non-HDL cholesterol is computed as Total Cholesterol - HDL. The TC:HDL ratio is computed as Total Cholesterol / HDL. The overall score counts how many of the four primary markers are in optimal range. This calculator does not implement the full Pooled Cohort Equations for 10-year ASCVD risk and does not account for family history, blood pressure, diabetes, smoking, or medication use.

A lipid panel measures four key blood fats: total cholesterol (desirable below 200 mg/dL), LDL or "bad" cholesterol (optimal below 100 mg/dL), HDL or "good" cholesterol (above 40 mg/dL for men, 50 mg/dL for women), and triglycerides (normal below 150 mg/dL). Non-HDL cholesterol (total minus HDL) captures all atherogenic particles and should be below 130 mg/dL. The TC:HDL ratio below 3.5 is optimal; above 5.0 indicates elevated risk. These thresholds come from the 2018 AHA/ACC guidelines and NCEP ATP III. Lifestyle changes including exercise, diet, and weight management can improve all four markers. Always consult your healthcare provider for personalized interpretation.
