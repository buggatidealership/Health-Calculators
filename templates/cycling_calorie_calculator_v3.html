How Many Calories Does Cycling Burn?

Cycling burns roughly 250–1,000 calories per hour, depending on your body weight and cycling intensity. A 155 lb (70 kg) person cycling at a moderate pace (12–14 mph) burns approximately 560 kcal/hour. Casual leisurely cycling burns around 280 kcal/hour, while racing pace (16–19 mph) can burn over 840 kcal/hour for the same person. Stationary bike at moderate effort burns around 385 kcal/hour — less than outdoor cycling at the same perceived intensity due to the absence of wind resistance.

How Cycling Intensity Affects Calorie Burn

Cycling at 14–16 mph burns roughly 2.5 times more calories per hour than casual <10 mph riding. The MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) for cycling ranges from 4.0 (leisurely) to 15.8 (elite race pace). Because calories = MET × body weight × hours, doubling your speed from 10 mph to 20 mph more than doubles your calorie burn — speed has a non-linear effect on energy expenditure because aerodynamic drag increases with the square of velocity.

The key factors driving cycling calorie burn are:

Primary Factors Affecting Cycling Calorie Burn

  • Speed / intensity: The single biggest factor. Racing pace (16–19 mph) burns 3× more than leisurely (<10 mph). Air resistance increases as the square of speed — above 15 mph, overcoming wind drag dominates energy expenditure.
  • Body weight: Heavier riders burn more calories at every intensity because more mass requires more energy to propel. A 205 lb rider burns ~33% more than a 155 lb rider at the same speed.
  • Duration: Calories scale linearly with time at a constant intensity — 60 minutes burns exactly twice as many calories as 30 minutes.
  • Terrain: Hills dramatically increase calorie burn — climbing requires significant additional energy to overcome gravity. This calculator uses general/flat MET values; actual burn on hilly terrain will be higher.
  • Bike type and fit: Aerodynamic road bikes are more efficient than mountain bikes for the same speed. A rider at 12 mph on a mountain bike works harder than on a road bike.

A 2013 study in the Journal of Science and Medicine in Sport (Peveler et al.) found that air resistance accounts for over 90% of total resistance at speeds above 15 mph on flat terrain — explaining why small increases in speed above 15 mph require exponentially more power and calorie expenditure.

Key Difference: Perceived Effort vs. Actual Calorie Burn

  • On a stationary bike, "moderate" effort typically corresponds to MET 5.5 — similar to road cycling at 10–12 mph.
  • Peloton/spin bikes with resistance can reach MET 8.5–10+ at high effort, matching vigorous outdoor cycling.
  • Recumbent bikes generally burn slightly fewer calories than upright bikes at the same resistance because the reclined position reduces core muscle engagement.
  • Heart rate monitors combined with power meters give the most accurate individual calorie estimates for cycling.
