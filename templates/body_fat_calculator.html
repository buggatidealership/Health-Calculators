What Is Body Fat Percentage?

Body fat percentage is the proportion of your total body weight that is fat tissue. Unlike BMI, which only uses height and weight, body fat percentage distinguishes between fat mass and lean mass (muscle, bone, organs, water). This calculator uses the U.S. Navy method — a circumference-based formula developed by Hodgdon and Beckett at the Naval Health Research Center — which estimates body fat from neck, waist, and hip measurements. It is distinct from the Army tape test, which uses different regression equations calibrated to Army fitness standards.

How Body Fat Percentage Is Calculated (Navy Method)

The U.S. Navy method uses logarithmic equations that relate circumference measurements to body density, which is then converted to body fat percentage. It was developed by Hodgdon and Beckett in 1984 at the Naval Health Research Center and validated against hydrostatic (underwater) weighing.

The Formulas

Men: BF% = 86.010 × log10(waist − neck) − 70.041 × log10(height) + 36.76

Women: BF% = 163.205 × log10(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log10(height) + 36.76

All measurements in centimeters. Imperial inputs are converted before calculation.

The key insight behind circumference-based methods is that waist size correlates strongly with visceral fat, while neck circumference inversely correlates with body fat. The logarithmic transformation accounts for the non-linear relationship between body dimensions and fat distribution.

In validation studies, the Navy method showed a standard error of estimate (SEE) of approximately 3.5% for men and 3.7% for women when compared to hydrostatic weighing, which was considered the reference standard at the time of development.

Body Fat Categories for Men and Women

The American Council on Exercise (ACE) defines five body fat categories. Healthy ranges differ significantly between men and women because women require more essential fat for reproductive function and hormonal health.
Category Men Women Description
Essential Fat 2–5% 10–13% Minimum needed for basic physiological function. Below this is dangerous.
Athletes 6–13% 14–20% Typical of competitive athletes and serious fitness enthusiasts.
Fitness 14–17% 21–24% Lean and healthy. Associated with good muscle definition and low disease risk.
Average 18–24% 25–31% Typical of the general population. Acceptable but not optimal.
Obese 25%+ 32%+ Elevated risk for cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.

Navy Method vs Other Methods

There are several ways to measure body fat, ranging from simple tape measurements to clinical imaging. Each has trade-offs in accuracy, cost, and accessibility.
Method Accuracy (SEE) Cost Notes
Navy Method (this calculator) ±3–4% Free Only needs a tape measure. Quick and repeatable.
DEXA Scan ±1–2% $75–200 Gold standard. Also measures bone density and regional fat distribution.
Skinfold Calipers ±3–4% $10–30 Accuracy depends heavily on the person taking measurements.
BIA (Bioelectrical Impedance) ±3–5% $20–100 Found in smart scales. Results vary with hydration and time of day.
Hydrostatic Weighing ±1.5–2.5% $40–75 Underwater weighing. Highly accurate but requires specialized equipment.

How to Measure Correctly

Accurate measurements are critical. Even a half-inch error can shift your body fat estimate by 1–2 percentage points. Use a flexible, non-elastic tape measure and follow these instructions carefully.

Neck Measurement

  • Measure just below the larynx (Adam's apple), perpendicular to the long axis of the neck.
  • Keep the tape level — do not angle it downward at the front.
  • Look straight ahead. Do not flex your neck.
  • Round down to the nearest half-inch (or nearest 0.5 cm).

Waist Measurement

  • Measure at the navel (belly button) level, horizontally around the abdomen.
  • Stand naturally. Breathe out normally — do not suck in your stomach.
  • Keep the tape snug against the skin without compressing.
  • Round up to the nearest half-inch (or nearest 0.5 cm).

Hip Measurement (Women Only)

  • Measure at the widest point of the buttocks / hips.
  • Stand with feet together and weight evenly distributed.
  • Keep the tape level and parallel to the floor.
  • Round up to the nearest half-inch (or nearest 0.5 cm).

Tips for Consistent Measurements

  • Measure at the same time of day (morning is best, before eating).
  • Have someone else take the measurements if possible.
  • Take each measurement twice and average the results.
  • Use the same tape measure each time.

Why Body Fat % Matters More Than BMI

BMI is a screening tool, not a body composition measurement. Body fat percentage provides a direct measure of how much of your weight is fat versus lean tissue, making it far more useful for health assessment.

BMI classifies people based solely on height and weight. This creates well-documented problems:

  • Muscular individuals are misclassified. A 5'10" man weighing 200 lbs has a BMI of 28.7 ("overweight") regardless of whether that weight is muscle or fat.
  • Older adults are under-classified. Age-related muscle loss (sarcopenia) means an older person can have a normal BMI but dangerously high body fat — sometimes called "normal weight obesity."
  • BMI ignores fat distribution. Visceral fat (around organs) is far more dangerous than subcutaneous fat (under skin). Body fat methods paired with waist measurements capture this distinction.

A 2016 study published in the Annals of Internal Medicine found that normal-weight individuals with central obesity (high waist-to-hip ratio) had the highest mortality risk — higher than those classified as obese by BMI. Body fat percentage, especially when combined with waist measurements as in the Navy method, provides more actionable health data than BMI alone.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the U.S. Navy body fat method?

The U.S. Navy method estimates body fat percentage using circumference measurements of the neck, waist, and hips (for women), along with height. Developed by Hodgdon and Beckett at the Naval Health Research Center in 1984, it uses logarithmic equations validated against hydrostatic weighing. All branches of the U.S. military use circumference-based methods for fitness assessments.

How accurate is the Navy method compared to DEXA?

The Navy method typically estimates body fat within 3–4 percentage points of DEXA scan results for most individuals. Accuracy decreases at extreme body fat levels (very low or very high). DEXA scans cost $75–200 and provide region-by-region fat distribution, which the Navy method cannot. For tracking changes over time, the Navy method is highly effective when measurements are taken consistently.

How is this different from the Army body fat calculator?

Both use circumference measurements, but they apply different mathematical formulas. The Navy method (Hodgdon-Beckett equations) and the Army tape test use different regression coefficients and were calibrated against different reference populations. Results typically differ by 1–3 percentage points. The Army calculator on this site uses Army-specific standards and compliance thresholds, while this calculator uses the Navy equations with ACE body fat classifications.

Where exactly should I measure my waist?

For the Navy method, measure your waist at the navel (belly button) level. Stand relaxed, exhale normally without sucking in your stomach, and wrap the tape horizontally around your abdomen. The tape should be snug but not compressing the skin. This differs from some medical guidelines that measure at the narrowest point of the torso.

Why is body fat percentage better than BMI?

BMI only considers height and weight, so it cannot distinguish between muscle and fat. A muscular athlete may have an "overweight" BMI but very low body fat. Conversely, an inactive person with low muscle mass may have a "normal" BMI but unhealthy body fat levels (normal weight obesity). Body fat percentage directly measures your fat-to-lean ratio, giving a more accurate picture of body composition and associated health risks.

What is a healthy body fat percentage?

According to the American Council on Exercise (ACE): Men — Essential fat 2–5%, Athletes 6–13%, Fitness 14–17%, Average 18–24%, Obese 25%+. Women — Essential fat 10–13%, Athletes 14–20%, Fitness 21–24%, Average 25–31%, Obese 32%+. The fitness range is generally considered optimal for long-term health and longevity.

Can I reduce body fat without losing weight?

Yes — this is called body recomposition. By strength training while eating at maintenance calories with adequate protein (1.6–2.2 g/kg body weight), you can simultaneously gain muscle and lose fat. Your scale weight may stay the same or even increase, but your body fat percentage will decrease. Tracking body fat rather than weight is essential for monitoring recomposition progress.
