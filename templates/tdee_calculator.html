What Is Total Daily Energy Expenditure?

Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is the total number of calories your body burns per day. It combines your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) — the energy needed for basic life-sustaining functions — with calories burned through physical activity, digestion, and non-exercise movement. This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which the American Dietetic Association recommends as the most accurate predictive formula for estimating caloric needs.

How the Mifflin-St Jeor Equation Works

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation calculates BMR using weight (kg), height (cm), age, and sex. Published in 1990, it is considered the gold standard for BMR estimation by the American Dietetic Association. Your TDEE is then calculated by multiplying BMR by an activity factor.

The Formula

Males: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) + 5

Females: BMR = (10 × weight in kg) + (6.25 × height in cm) − (5 × age) − 161

TDEE = BMR × Activity Factor

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation replaced the older Harris-Benedict equation (1919) because it better reflects modern body compositions. A 2005 validation study by the ADA found it predicts BMR within ±10% for 82% of individuals — the highest accuracy among non-calorimetry methods.

Activity Level Guide

Activity factors range from 1.2 (sedentary) to 1.9 (extra active) and account for all movement beyond basal metabolism, including exercise, walking, fidgeting, and occupational activity. Most desk workers overestimate their activity level — if unsure, choose one level lower than you think.
Level Factor Description
Sedentary 1.2 Desk job, no planned exercise
Lightly Active 1.375 Light exercise 1-3 days/week or daily walking
Moderately Active 1.55 Moderate exercise 3-5 days/week
Very Active 1.725 Hard exercise 6-7 days/week
Extra Active 1.9 Very hard exercise + physical job or 2x training

Frequently Asked Questions

What is TDEE?

TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure — the total number of calories your body burns per day, combining your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) with calories burned through physical activity, digestion (thermic effect of food), and non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT).

How accurate is the Mifflin-St Jeor equation?

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation predicts BMR within 10% of measured values for approximately 82% of individuals, making it the most accurate predictive equation recommended by the American Dietetic Association. However, factors like muscle mass, genetics, and metabolic conditions can cause individual variation.

How do I use TDEE for weight loss?

To lose weight, eat fewer calories than your TDEE. The NIH recommends a deficit of 500-1,000 kcal/day for safe weight loss of 1-2 lbs (0.5-1 kg) per week. Avoid going below 1,200 kcal/day (women) or 1,500 kcal/day (men) without medical supervision.

What's the difference between BMR and TDEE?

BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate) is the number of calories your body burns at complete rest to maintain vital functions like breathing, circulation, and cell production. TDEE adds the calories you burn through physical activity, the thermic effect of food (~10% of intake), and non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) to your BMR. For most people, BMR accounts for 60-75% of TDEE.

Should I eat back exercise calories?

If you selected an activity level that includes your exercise, your TDEE already accounts for those calories — don't add them again. If you chose "Sedentary" and track exercise separately, you can add a portion (about 50-75%) of burned calories, as exercise trackers tend to overestimate.

How often should I recalculate TDEE?

Recalculate every 10 lbs (4.5 kg) of weight change, or every 4-6 weeks during active dieting. As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases — a phenomenon called metabolic adaptation. Failing to recalculate is a common reason weight loss plateaus.
