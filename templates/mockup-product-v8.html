Frequently Asked Questions

  • BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic one. It measures weight relative to height but cannot distinguish between muscle mass, bone density, and fat. Athletes may have a high BMI with low body fat. BMI is most useful as a starting point for a broader health assessment.

  • A BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is generally considered a healthy range for adults. However, healthy ranges can vary by age, sex, ethnicity, and muscle mass. The healthy range for older adults may be slightly higher (up to 27) as some research suggests a slightly higher BMI is protective in aging.

  • For adults, standard BMI categories apply regardless of sex. However, women typically have more body fat than men at the same BMI. For children and teens (ages 2-19), BMI is expressed as a percentile relative to others of the same age and sex, because body composition changes significantly during growth.

  • Both are useful but measure different things. BMI is simple and requires only height and weight. Body fat percentage is more precise but requires specialized equipment (DEXA scan, calipers, bioimpedance). For most people, BMI is a reasonable first approximation. If you exercise regularly or have unusual body composition, body fat percentage will give you a clearer picture.

  • BMI = weight (kg) / height (m) squared. In imperial units: BMI = (weight in pounds x 703) / (height in inches squared). The formula was devised by Belgian mathematician Adolphe Quetelet in the 1830s and remains the standard screening metric used worldwide.
