About Cholesterol Ratios

  • Total/HDL Ratio: Total cholesterol divided by HDL. The AHA considers <5 desirable. Below 3.5 is optimal.
  • LDL/HDL Ratio: LDL divided by HDL. Optimal is below 2.0. Values above 3.5 indicate elevated risk.
  • Non-HDL Cholesterol: Total cholesterol minus HDL. Captures all atherogenic lipoproteins (LDL + VLDL). ATP III target: <130 mg/dL for most adults.
  • Triglyceride/HDL Ratio: A proxy marker for insulin resistance and small dense LDL particles. Optimal is below 2.0; above 3.0 suggests metabolic risk.
  • Why ratios matter: Individual cholesterol numbers don't tell the full story. A high total cholesterol with high HDL may be lower risk than a lower total cholesterol with very low HDL.

What Is a Cholesterol Ratio?

A cholesterol ratio compares different types of cholesterol in your blood to assess cardiovascular risk more accurately than any single number alone. The most commonly used is the Total Cholesterol to HDL ratio, which the American Heart Association identifies as a useful predictor of heart disease risk. A ratio below 5 is considered desirable, while below 3.5 is considered optimal.

Your doctor may order a standard lipid panel that reports total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, and triglycerides as individual numbers. But these numbers gain context when viewed as ratios. For example, a total cholesterol of 240 mg/dL with an HDL of 80 mg/dL gives a ratio of 3.0 (optimal), while a total of 200 mg/dL with an HDL of 35 mg/dL gives a ratio of 5.7 (elevated risk) — even though the total cholesterol appears "better."

Understanding Each Cholesterol Ratio

Total Cholesterol / HDL Ratio

This is the most widely used cholesterol ratio. The American Heart Association considers it a useful number for assessing cardiovascular risk. It works because it captures the balance between all the cholesterol in your blood and the HDL particles that remove cholesterol from arteries.

  • Optimal: Below 3.5 — associated with significantly lower heart disease risk
  • Desirable: 3.5 to 5.0 — considered acceptable by AHA standards
  • Borderline high: 5.0 to 6.0 — elevated cardiovascular risk
  • High risk: Above 6.0 — roughly double the average heart disease risk

LDL / HDL Ratio

This ratio directly compares the "bad" cholesterol (LDL, which deposits cholesterol in artery walls) against the "good" cholesterol (HDL, which removes it). The Framingham Heart Study found that the LDL/HDL ratio was a strong predictor of coronary heart disease events.

  • Optimal: Below 2.0
  • Desirable: 2.0 to 2.5
  • Borderline: 2.5 to 3.5
  • High risk: Above 3.5 (men) / Above 3.0 (women)

Non-HDL Cholesterol

Non-HDL cholesterol is simply your total cholesterol minus HDL. It captures all the "bad" cholesterol types — not just LDL, but also VLDL and other atherogenic particles. The ATP III guidelines from the National Cholesterol Education Program identified non-HDL as a secondary target of therapy, particularly when triglycerides are elevated (above 200 mg/dL), because in that situation LDL alone can underestimate atherogenic burden.

Triglyceride / HDL Ratio

This ratio has gained attention as a proxy marker for insulin resistance and the presence of small, dense LDL particles — the most dangerous type. A 2019 study in the journal Lipids in Health and Disease found that TG/HDL ratio was an independent predictor of cardiovascular events. A high ratio (above 3.0 in mg/dL units) suggests a pattern of metabolic dysfunction even when LDL appears normal.

How to Improve Your Cholesterol Ratios

Because ratios involve two numbers, you can improve them by lowering bad cholesterol, raising good cholesterol, or both. Here are evidence-based strategies:

Raise HDL (Improves All Ratios)

  • Exercise regularly: 150+ minutes/week of moderate aerobic activity can raise HDL by 5-10%. The effect is dose-dependent — more vigorous exercise produces larger increases.
  • Quit smoking: Smoking lowers HDL by 4-6 mg/dL on average. HDL typically increases within weeks of quitting.
  • Moderate alcohol: 1 drink/day for women, 1-2 for men is associated with higher HDL, but the AHA does not recommend starting drinking for this purpose.
  • Lose excess weight: Every 6 lbs lost raises HDL by approximately 1 mg/dL.
  • Choose healthy fats: Replace saturated and trans fats with monounsaturated fats (olive oil, avocados, nuts).

Lower LDL (Improves LDL/HDL and Non-HDL)

  • Reduce saturated fat: Replace with unsaturated fats. Reducing saturated fat from 14% to 7% of calories typically lowers LDL by 10-15%.
  • Add soluble fiber: 5-10g/day from oats, beans, and barley can lower LDL by 5-10%.
  • Plant sterols/stanols: 2g/day (from fortified foods or supplements) can lower LDL by 6-15%.
  • Statins: If lifestyle changes are insufficient, statins can lower LDL by 30-50%. Discuss with your doctor.

Lower Triglycerides (Improves Trig/HDL)

  • Reduce sugar and refined carbs: These directly increase triglyceride production in the liver.
  • Limit alcohol: Even moderate alcohol can significantly raise triglycerides in some people.
  • Eat omega-3 fatty acids: 2-4g/day of EPA+DHA from fish oil can reduce triglycerides by 25-30%.
  • Lose weight: A 5-10% weight reduction can lower triglycerides by 20-30%.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most important cholesterol ratio?

The Total Cholesterol / HDL ratio is the most widely used and validated. The American Heart Association recognizes it as a useful predictor of heart disease risk. However, many cardiologists now consider Non-HDL cholesterol a better single metric than any ratio, because it captures all atherogenic particles and is included in the 2018 AHA/ACC cholesterol guidelines as a treatment target.

What cholesterol ratio is considered dangerous?

A Total/HDL ratio above 6.0 is considered high risk — roughly double the average heart disease risk. An LDL/HDL ratio above 3.5 (men) or 3.0 (women) is also considered elevated. For context, the average American man has a Total/HDL ratio of about 5.0 and the average American woman about 4.4, so "average" does not mean "optimal."

Is cholesterol ratio more important than individual numbers?

Both matter. The 2018 AHA/ACC guidelines primarily use LDL cholesterol as the treatment target, not ratios. However, ratios add context: a person with LDL of 130 and HDL of 70 (LDL/HDL = 1.86) is at lower risk than someone with LDL of 130 and HDL of 35 (LDL/HDL = 3.71). Your doctor will consider ratios alongside absolute values, age, blood pressure, diabetes status, and other risk factors.

What does the triglyceride/HDL ratio tell you?

The triglyceride/HDL ratio is a proxy marker for insulin resistance and the presence of small, dense LDL particles — a particularly harmful subtype not captured by standard LDL measurement. A ratio above 3.0 (in mg/dL) suggests metabolic dysfunction and increased cardiovascular risk, even if your LDL appears normal. This ratio is sometimes called a "poor man's insulin resistance test."

How do I calculate non-HDL cholesterol?

Non-HDL cholesterol = Total Cholesterol minus HDL. For example, if your total cholesterol is 210 mg/dL and your HDL is 55 mg/dL, your non-HDL is 155 mg/dL. The ATP III guidelines recommend non-HDL below 130 mg/dL for most adults, with a goal of below 100 mg/dL for high-risk patients. Non-HDL has the advantage of not requiring a fasting blood draw, unlike LDL calculated by the Friedewald equation.

Can I calculate my cholesterol ratio at home?

You can calculate the ratios from any lipid panel results. The math is simple division. However, you need accurate lipid panel numbers, which require a blood test ordered by your doctor. Home cholesterol test kits exist but are less accurate than laboratory tests. The calculator above does the ratio math for you once you have your lab results.
