Frequently Asked Questions

  • A complete sleep cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes and includes four stages: light sleep (N1, N2), deep sleep (N3), and REM sleep. Most adults complete 4-6 cycles per night.

  • Grogginess (sleep inertia) occurs when your alarm disrupts deep sleep (N3). Timing your alarm to the end of a cycle means waking during lighter sleep, reducing grogginess.

  • The National Sleep Foundation recommends 7-9 hours for adults (18-64). Individual needs vary. If you feel refreshed and alert without caffeine, you are likely getting enough.

  • 7.5 hours (5 complete cycles) is better for most adults. Chronically sleeping 6 hours impairs cognitive performance equivalently to two nights of total sleep deprivation.

  • No. Hitting snooze fragments sleep into intervals too short for any meaningful sleep stage. This increases grogginess. Set your alarm for the latest possible cycle-end time.

Methodology

This calculator uses the 90-minute sleep cycle model. It takes your wake-up time, subtracts fall-asleep time, then calculates bedtimes for 3-6 complete cycles. Age-specific recommendations are from the NSF expert panel (Hirshkowitz et al., 2015). Individual cycle lengths vary from 80-110 minutes.

Sleep cycles last approximately 90 minutes, progressing through light sleep (N1, N2), deep sleep (N3), and REM sleep. Waking at the end of a complete cycle reduces grogginess. For a 7 AM wake time with 15 minutes to fall asleep, optimal bedtimes are: 11:15 PM (5 cycles, 7.5h), 9:45 PM (6 cycles, 9h), or 12:45 AM (4 cycles, 6h). Adults need 7-9 hours per night.
