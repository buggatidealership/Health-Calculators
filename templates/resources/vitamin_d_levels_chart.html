Vitamin D Blood Level Ranges

A 25-hydroxyvitamin D level between 30 and 50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L) is considered sufficient by the Endocrine Society, while the optimal range for overall health is 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L).1 The standard blood test for vitamin D status measures serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D], which reflects both dietary intake and cutaneous synthesis from UVB exposure. This metabolite has a circulating half-life of approximately 2-3 weeks, making it the most reliable biomarker for vitamin D stores.2

Two major bodies define the clinical thresholds differently. The Institute of Medicine (IOM) considers 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) adequate for bone health in 97.5% of the population.3 The Endocrine Society uses a higher threshold of 30 ng/mL (75 nmol/L) as the minimum for sufficiency, reflecting evidence for extraskeletal benefits including immune function, cardiovascular health, and cancer risk reduction.1

Vitamin D Blood Level Classification (25-hydroxyvitamin D)
Status ng/mL nmol/L Clinical Significance
Severely Deficient <10 <25 Rickets (children), osteomalacia (adults), severe muscle weakness
Deficient 10-20 25-50 Increased bone loss, elevated fracture risk, impaired calcium absorption
Insufficient 20-30 50-75 Suboptimal bone health, possible immune and metabolic effects
Sufficient 30-50 75-125 Adequate for bone and general health (Endocrine Society threshold)
Optimal 40-60 100-150 Associated with best outcomes for immune, cardiovascular, and bone health1
Potentially Harmful >100 >250 Risk of hypercalcemia, kidney stones, soft tissue calcification4

The conversion factor between units is: 1 ng/mL = 2.5 nmol/L. Labs in the United States typically report results in ng/mL, while many international labs use nmol/L. Use our Vitamin D Conversion Calculator for instant conversions between the two units.

It is important to note that the "optimal" range of 40-60 ng/mL is supported by observational studies showing associations with reduced risk of autoimmune disease, certain cancers, and cardiovascular events, but randomized controlled trials have not definitively established causation for all extraskeletal outcomes.5 The IOM's lower threshold of 20 ng/mL is based on established evidence for bone health specifically.

Recommended Daily Intake by Age

The recommended daily vitamin D intake varies by age, with the Endocrine Society consistently recommending higher doses than the IOM to achieve serum levels above 30 ng/mL.13 Both organizations agree on a tolerable upper intake level (UL) of 4,000 IU/day for anyone over age 9, above which the risk of adverse effects increases without medical supervision.

Vitamin D Daily Intake Recommendations (IU/day)
Age Group IOM RDA Endocrine Society Tolerable Upper Limit
Infants 0-12 months 400 IU 400-1,000 IU 1,000-2,000 IU
Children 1-18 years 600 IU 600-1,000 IU 2,500-4,000 IU*
Adults 19-70 years 600 IU 1,500-2,000 IU 4,000 IU
Adults 71+ years 800 IU 1,500-2,000 IU 4,000 IU
Pregnant & Lactating 600 IU 1,500-2,000 IU 4,000 IU

*UL varies by age within this group: 2,500 IU for ages 1-3, 3,000 IU for ages 4-8, and 4,000 IU for ages 9-18.3

The Endocrine Society's 2011 Clinical Practice Guideline notes that obese individuals (BMI ≥ 30) may require 2-3 times more vitamin D to achieve the same serum levels as normal-weight individuals, because vitamin D is sequestered in adipose tissue.1 Individuals on medications that accelerate vitamin D metabolism (e.g., anticonvulsants, glucocorticoids, antiretrovirals) may also need higher doses.

Vitamin D Deficiency by Country

An estimated 1 billion people worldwide have vitamin D deficiency or insufficiency, making it one of the most common nutritional deficiencies globally.6 Prevalence varies dramatically by region, driven by differences in latitude, skin pigmentation, dietary patterns, cultural clothing practices, and public health fortification policies.

Estimated Vitamin D Deficiency Prevalence by Country/Region (<20 ng/mL)
Country / Region Estimated Deficiency (%) Key Contributing Factors
United States ~42% Indoor lifestyles, obesity, racial disparities (82% of Black adults)7
United Kingdom ~40% High latitude (50-60°N), limited UVB October-March8
Germany ~50% Northern latitude, low supplementation rates
India ~70-80% Dark skin pigmentation, air pollution blocking UVB, low dairy intake9
Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Iran, UAE) ~60-80% Concealing clothing, extreme heat limiting outdoor activity, limited fortification6
Australia ~23% Strong sun-safety culture, sunscreen use; lower than expected for latitude
Japan ~40-50% Cultural preference for pale skin, sun avoidance, limited fortification
Brazil ~28% Despite tropical latitude, high urbanization and indoor work drive deficiency
Canada ~37% High latitude, long winters; mandatory vitamin D milk fortification helps
Nordic Countries (Finland, Sweden, Norway) ~25-40% High latitude but strong supplementation culture and food fortification policies

Notably, some of the highest deficiency rates are found not in northern countries but in South Asia and the Middle East, where factors beyond latitude -- including skin pigmentation, cultural dress, and limited food fortification -- play a dominant role.6 Countries with mandatory vitamin D food fortification programs (Finland, Canada, United States) tend to have lower deficiency rates than their latitude alone would predict.

Symptoms of Vitamin D Deficiency

Vitamin D deficiency often develops silently: many individuals with levels of 10-20 ng/mL have no obvious symptoms, which is why routine screening is recommended for high-risk groups.1 When symptoms do appear, they typically reflect impaired calcium metabolism, weakened immune function, or disrupted neuromuscular signaling.

Skeletal System

  • Bone pain and tenderness -- particularly in the lower back, pelvis, and legs, caused by inadequate bone mineralization (osteomalacia)2
  • Increased fracture risk -- vitamin D deficiency reduces calcium absorption to 10-15% (vs. 30-40% at sufficient levels), accelerating bone density loss3
  • Rickets in children -- skeletal deformities (bowed legs, widened wrists) at levels below 10 ng/mL

Muscular System

  • Proximal muscle weakness -- difficulty climbing stairs, rising from a chair, or lifting objects overhead
  • Muscle cramps and spasms -- related to impaired calcium and magnesium regulation
  • Increased fall risk in elderly -- meta-analyses show vitamin D supplementation reduces falls by 19% in deficient individuals10

Immune System

  • Frequent respiratory infections -- vitamin D activates antimicrobial peptides (cathelicidin, defensins) in immune cells5
  • Slow wound healing -- vitamin D modulates inflammatory cytokines involved in tissue repair
  • Autoimmune associations -- low levels linked to higher incidence of multiple sclerosis, type 1 diabetes, and rheumatoid arthritis in observational studies

Neurological & Psychological

  • Fatigue and daytime sleepiness -- one of the most commonly reported symptoms, often improving with repletion
  • Depression and low mood -- vitamin D receptors are present in brain regions involved in mood regulation; meta-analyses suggest supplementation has a small positive effect on depressive symptoms11
  • Cognitive impairment in elderly -- severe deficiency associated with increased dementia risk in observational studies

How to Raise Your Vitamin D Levels

Each 1,000 IU of daily vitamin D3 supplementation raises serum 25(OH)D by approximately 10 ng/mL (25 nmol/L) over 2-3 months, though the response varies by body weight, baseline level, and genetic factors.1 The table below provides general dosing guidance based on your current blood level and target range.

Supplementation Guidance by Current Vitamin D Level
Current Level (ng/mL) Target Level Suggested Daily D3 Dose Expected Timeline
<10 (Severe) 40-60 ng/mL 5,000-10,000 IU (loading) then 2,000-4,000 IU maintenance* 3-4 months
10-20 (Deficient) 40-60 ng/mL 4,000-5,000 IU (loading) then 1,500-2,000 IU maintenance 2-3 months
20-30 (Insufficient) 40-60 ng/mL 2,000-4,000 IU daily 8-12 weeks
30-40 (Sufficient) 40-60 ng/mL 1,000-2,000 IU daily 6-8 weeks
40-60 (Optimal) Maintain 1,000-2,000 IU daily (maintenance) Ongoing

*Doses above 4,000 IU/day should be taken under medical supervision with periodic blood level monitoring. An alternative loading protocol is 50,000 IU once weekly for 8 weeks, commonly prescribed by physicians for severe deficiency.1

Three Sources of Vitamin D

Sunlight (UVB radiation): The body synthesizes vitamin D3 when UVB rays (290-315 nm wavelength) strike the skin. Fair-skinned individuals produce approximately 10,000-20,000 IU after 10-15 minutes of midday summer sun exposure on arms and legs.2 Darker skin requires 3-6 times longer for equivalent production. UVB availability drops significantly above 35°N latitude during winter months.

Dietary sources: Few foods naturally contain significant vitamin D. Fatty fish (salmon: ~600-1,000 IU per 3.5 oz serving; mackerel: ~400 IU) and cod liver oil (~1,360 IU per tablespoon) are the richest natural sources. Fortified milk typically provides 100-120 IU per cup. Egg yolks contain 40-50 IU each.3

Supplements: Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) supplements are the most effective way to reliably raise and maintain blood levels. Available as daily tablets (1,000-5,000 IU), weekly capsules (50,000 IU), and liquid drops. Take with a fat-containing meal to maximize absorption, as vitamin D is fat-soluble.

Vitamin D2 vs D3: Which Is Better?

Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is generally more effective than D2 (ergocalciferol) at raising and maintaining serum 25(OH)D levels. A 2012 meta-analysis found that D3 was 87% more potent than D2 in raising serum 25(OH)D concentrations and produced 2-3 times greater storage of the vitamin.12

Vitamin D2 vs D3 Comparison
Property Vitamin D2 (Ergocalciferol) Vitamin D3 (Cholecalciferol)
Source Fungi, yeast, UV-irradiated mushrooms Animal products, lanolin, lichen (vegan D3), skin synthesis
Potency (raising 25(OH)D) Lower (baseline reference) ~87% more effective than D212
Half-life in circulation ~2-3 days ~2-3 weeks
Binding to vitamin D-binding protein Lower affinity Higher affinity (longer retention)
Stability Less stable (degrades faster in storage) More stable
Suitable for vegans? Yes (plant/fungal origin) Only lichen-derived D3 is vegan
Prescription form 50,000 IU capsules (common Rx) Available OTC in most doses; Rx 50,000 IU less common
Cost Generally cheaper Slightly more expensive, widely available

Bottom line: For most people, D3 is the preferred form due to its greater potency and longer duration of action. Strict vegans who cannot use lichen-derived D3 may use D2, but should be aware that more frequent dosing or higher amounts may be needed to achieve the same blood levels. Both forms are effective for treating severe deficiency when used at appropriate doses.1

Who Is Most at Risk for Deficiency?

Certain populations face significantly elevated risk of vitamin D deficiency due to reduced sun exposure, impaired synthesis, or increased physiological demand.16 The following table summarizes major risk factors and the mechanism by which they lower vitamin D status.

Testing Your Vitamin D Level

The 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] test is the standard clinical assay for assessing vitamin D status.2 This test measures the total concentration of both 25(OH)D2 and 25(OH)D3 in serum, providing a comprehensive picture of vitamin D from all sources -- diet, supplements, and sun exposure.

Key Facts About the 25(OH)D Test

  • Test name: 25-hydroxyvitamin D (also written as 25(OH)D or calcidiol)
  • Sample type: Venous blood draw (serum or plasma)
  • Fasting required? No, fasting is not necessary
  • Turnaround time: Typically 1-3 business days
  • Units: ng/mL (US) or nmol/L (international); 1 ng/mL = 2.5 nmol/L
  • Cost: $40-$80 without insurance in the US; often covered for at-risk populations

Important distinction: Do not confuse the 25(OH)D test with the 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D test (calcitriol). The 1,25(OH)2D test measures the active hormonal form, which has a very short half-life (4-6 hours) and is tightly regulated by parathyroid hormone. It does not reliably reflect vitamin D stores and is only ordered for specific conditions such as hypercalcemia, sarcoidosis, or renal failure.2

When to Test

The Endocrine Society recommends 25(OH)D testing for individuals at risk for deficiency, including those with osteoporosis, chronic kidney disease, malabsorption syndromes, obesity, darker skin, and older adults with a history of falls.1 Routine population screening is not recommended by the IOM for healthy individuals at low risk. After starting supplementation for deficiency, recheck levels at 8-12 weeks to confirm response to treatment.

Timing note: Vitamin D levels fluctuate seasonally. If tested in summer, levels may be 10-20 ng/mL higher than winter values at the same supplementation dose, due to cutaneous synthesis from sun exposure. For the most clinically useful result, consider testing in late winter (February-March in the Northern Hemisphere) when levels are at their nadir.

Methodology

Blood level classifications and recommended daily intake values in this chart are drawn from the Endocrine Society Clinical Practice Guideline on vitamin D (Holick et al., 2011) and the Institute of Medicine Dietary Reference Intakes for Calcium and Vitamin D (2011). Global deficiency prevalence data is sourced from peer-reviewed epidemiological studies indexed in PubMed, including national surveys from the U.S. (NHANES), UK, and India. All threshold values distinguish between the more conservative IOM recommendations (designed for general population bone health) and the Endocrine Society guidelines (targeted at patients at risk for deficiency), and discrepancies between these two frameworks are noted throughout.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a normal vitamin D level in ng/mL?

A normal (sufficient) vitamin D level is 30-50 ng/mL (75-125 nmol/L) according to the Endocrine Society. The Institute of Medicine considers 20 ng/mL (50 nmol/L) adequate for bone health. Levels below 20 ng/mL are classified as deficient, and below 10 ng/mL as severely deficient. The optimal range for overall health is 40-60 ng/mL (100-150 nmol/L).

How much vitamin D should I take daily?

The IOM recommends 600 IU/day for adults aged 19-70 and 800 IU/day for adults over 70. The Endocrine Society recommends 1,500-2,000 IU/day for adults to maintain levels above 30 ng/mL. The tolerable upper intake level is 4,000 IU/day for adults, though some clinicians prescribe higher doses under supervision for correcting deficiency.

What percentage of people are vitamin D deficient?

Approximately 42% of US adults have vitamin D levels below 20 ng/mL. Globally, an estimated 1 billion people have vitamin D deficiency or insufficiency. Rates are highest in the Middle East (60-80%), South Asia (70-80%), and Northern Europe (40-60%).

Is vitamin D3 better than vitamin D2?

Yes. Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) is approximately 87% more potent than D2 (ergocalciferol) at raising serum 25(OH)D levels. D3 also has a longer half-life in circulation (2-3 weeks vs. 2-3 days for D2). For most people, D3 is the preferred supplementation form. Vegans can choose lichen-derived D3 or use D2 at higher doses.

What are the symptoms of low vitamin D?

Common symptoms include bone pain, muscle weakness, fatigue, depression, impaired wound healing, and frequent infections. Severe deficiency (below 10 ng/mL) can cause osteomalacia in adults and rickets in children. Many people with mild deficiency (10-20 ng/mL) may have no obvious symptoms.

How long does it take to correct vitamin D deficiency?

It typically takes 8-12 weeks of supplementation to correct vitamin D deficiency. A common loading protocol is 50,000 IU once weekly for 8 weeks, followed by maintenance of 1,500-2,000 IU daily. Each 1,000 IU of daily vitamin D3 raises blood levels by approximately 10 ng/mL over 2-3 months.

Can you take too much vitamin D?

Yes. Vitamin D toxicity can occur at blood levels above 100 ng/mL (250 nmol/L), though it is rare and typically requires prolonged intake above 10,000 IU/day. Symptoms include nausea, vomiting, weakness, and dangerously elevated blood calcium. The tolerable upper intake level is 4,000 IU/day for adults. Toxicity does not occur from sun exposure alone.

What is the best test for vitamin D levels?

The 25-hydroxyvitamin D [25(OH)D] blood test is the gold standard. It reflects vitamin D from both diet and sun exposure, with a half-life of 2-3 weeks. Do not confuse it with the 1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D test, which measures the active hormonal form and does not reliably reflect vitamin D stores.

Does sunscreen block vitamin D production?

In theory, SPF 30 blocks about 97% of UVB rays needed for vitamin D synthesis. However, real-world studies show regular sunscreen use rarely causes vitamin D deficiency because people typically do not apply enough to block all UVB, and incidental exposure on uncovered skin still contributes. The Endocrine Society recommends 10-15 minutes of unprotected midday sun on arms and legs 2-3 times per week for fair-skinned individuals.
