How the Calculator Works

This calculator uses weight loss percentages derived from the STEP and SUSTAIN clinical trial programs. It factors in your starting weight, BMI, planned dosage, and treatment duration to estimate total weight loss based on dose-specific outcomes observed in published research.

Clinical Trial Basis

2.4 mg (Wegovy dose): Average 14.9% body weight loss at 68 weeks (STEP 1, N=1,961)

1.0 mg (higher Ozempic dose): Average ~8% body weight loss at 52 weeks (SUSTAIN trials)

0.5 mg (standard Ozempic dose): Average ~5% body weight loss at 52 weeks (SUSTAIN trials)

The calculator scales these percentages proportionally to your selected treatment duration. Actual weight loss trajectories are non-linear — most people lose weight fastest between weeks 12 and 28 during dose escalation — but the total projected loss at your selected endpoint reflects the clinical averages.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much weight can I lose on Ozempic?

In the STEP 1 clinical trial (N=1,961), participants taking semaglutide 2.4 mg weekly lost an average of 14.9% of their body weight over 68 weeks, compared to 2.4% with placebo. At the standard Ozempic doses (0.5 mg and 1.0 mg), the SUSTAIN trials showed weight loss typically ranges from 5% to 10% of body weight over 6-12 months. Results depend on dosage, treatment duration, baseline BMI, and whether you combine the medication with dietary changes and physical activity.

When will I start seeing results?

Most people start noticing weight loss within 4-6 weeks of starting treatment. In the STEP trials, participants showed measurable weight reduction by week 4, with weight loss continuing progressively through week 68. The rate of loss is typically fastest between weeks 12 and 28 during dose escalation, then gradually slows as the body approaches a new equilibrium. During the initial dose-titration phase (weeks 1-16), the primary goal is tolerability rather than maximum weight loss.

Should I diet while taking Ozempic?

Yes. All STEP clinical trials combined semaglutide with a reduced-calorie diet (500 kcal/day deficit below estimated needs) and increased physical activity (150 minutes/week of moderate exercise). In STEP 2, participants with type 2 diabetes who combined semaglutide 2.4 mg with lifestyle intervention lost significantly more weight than those on medication alone. Semaglutide reduces appetite and food cravings — particularly for high-fat foods — making it easier to sustain a calorie deficit, but dietary changes amplify the results substantially.

Will I regain weight after stopping Ozempic?

The STEP 4 trial specifically studied this question. Participants who switched from semaglutide 2.4 mg to placebo after 20 weeks of treatment regained approximately two-thirds of the weight they had lost over the following 48 weeks. They also experienced increases in waist circumference, HbA1c, and lipid levels. Maintaining diet and exercise habits after discontinuation can reduce the extent of regain, but most people experience some weight rebound because the appetite-suppressing and metabolic effects of semaglutide cease when the medication is stopped.

Is this calculator based on real clinical trials?

Yes. The weight loss projections are derived from the STEP (Semaglutide Treatment Effect in People with obesity) and SUSTAIN clinical trial programs. STEP 1 was published in the New England Journal of Medicine (Wilding et al., 2021, N=1,961), STEP 2 in The Lancet (Davies et al., 2021), and STEP 4 in JAMA (Rubino et al., 2021). These trials enrolled thousands of participants across multiple countries and represent the strongest available evidence for semaglutide weight loss outcomes.

Methodology

This calculator projects weight loss using dose-specific percentage outcomes from published clinical trials. For the 2.4 mg dose, projections are based on the STEP 1 trial (Wilding et al., NEJM 2021, N=1,961), which showed 14.9% average body weight loss at 68 weeks. For the 1.0 mg and 0.5 mg doses, projections use data from the SUSTAIN trial program, showing approximately 8% and 5% weight loss at 52 weeks, respectively. Weight loss is calculated as a percentage of starting body weight and scaled proportionally to the selected treatment duration, with a cap to prevent unrealistic extrapolation beyond trial endpoints.

All STEP trials combined semaglutide with a reduced-calorie diet (500 kcal/day deficit) and 150 minutes/week of physical activity. Individual results vary significantly based on diet adherence, exercise habits, metabolic health, and genetics. The calculator does not account for the non-linear trajectory of weight loss (fastest during weeks 12-28), dose titration timing, or the presence of type 2 diabetes, which typically reduces weight loss outcomes. This tool provides estimates only and does not replace clinical guidance.

Who Should Not Use Semaglutide (Contraindications)

Semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy) is a prescription medication with specific contraindications. The following is based on FDA-approved prescribing information from Novo Nordisk. Do not use this calculator as a substitute for a clinical evaluation by your prescribing physician.

Absolute Contraindications (Do Not Use):

  • Personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma (MTC) — semaglutide caused thyroid C-cell tumors in rodent studies. The FDA label carries a boxed warning for this risk.
  • Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia syndrome type 2 (MEN 2) — patients with MEN 2 are at elevated risk for MTC and should not take any GLP-1 receptor agonist.
  • Known hypersensitivity to semaglutide or any excipients — serious hypersensitivity reactions including anaphylaxis and angioedema have been reported.

Serious but Rare Adverse Events

  • Pancreatitis — acute pancreatitis has been reported. Discontinue if suspected (persistent severe abdominal pain).
  • Gallbladder disease — cholelithiasis and cholecystitis occurred at higher rates in semaglutide groups than placebo in STEP trials.
  • Acute kidney injury — reported in patients with nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea leading to dehydration. Maintain adequate hydration.
  • Suicidal ideation — the FDA issued a safety communication in 2023 reviewing reports of suicidal thoughts. The European Medicines Agency concluded no causal link in their 2023 review, but monitoring is recommended for patients with a history of psychiatric disorders.

Side effects are most common during dose escalation and tend to diminish as the body adjusts. The standard dose titration schedule (starting at 0.25 mg and increasing every 4 weeks) is specifically designed to minimize gastrointestinal intolerance. In STEP 1, 7% of participants on semaglutide discontinued treatment due to gastrointestinal adverse events, compared to 3% on placebo.

Important: Report any new or worsening symptoms to your prescribing physician. Do not adjust your dose or discontinue semaglutide without medical guidance. If you experience persistent severe abdominal pain, signs of an allergic reaction (swelling, difficulty breathing), or changes in mood or thoughts of self-harm, seek medical attention immediately.
