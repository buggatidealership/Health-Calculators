Three steps. Every calculator. Every time.

Step 01

AI reads the paper

Pulse scans peer-reviewed research from PubMed, NIH, and WHO. It identifies the validated clinical formula — not blog posts, not guesswork, not "one doctor said."

For TDEE, that's the Mifflin-St Jeor equation — recommended by the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics as the most accurate predictor of basal metabolic rate.

A new predictive equation for resting energy expenditure in healthy individuals

"...the equation developed, 10 × weight (kg) + 6.25 × height (cm) − 5 × age (y) + s, where s is +5 for males and −161 for females, was more accurate than the Harris-Benedict equation..."

Mifflin MD et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 1990;51(2):241-247.
Step 02

Extracts the formula

The validated equation is isolated, parameterized, and tested against known inputs. Edge cases are handled — minimum ages, maximum weights, activity multiplier bounds.

The formula becomes code. The code becomes a calculator. The citation travels with it — always.

Extracted formula
BMR = 10 × weightkg + 6.25 × heightcm − 5 × age + s
where s = +5 (male) or −161 (female)

TDEE = BMR × activity multiplier
Validated against source

Builds the calculator

The formula becomes an instant tool. Your inputs go in, your number comes out — with context. Where do you fall? What does it mean? What should you do next?
