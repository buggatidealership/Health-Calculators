Frequently Asked Questions

  • 10,000 steps burns approximately 300-500 calories for most adults. For a 155-lb (70 kg) person walking briskly (3.0 mph), the figure is about 400 calories. Lighter individuals burn less; heavier individuals burn more.

  • Burning 500 calories from walking typically requires 12,000-17,000 steps, depending on your weight and pace. For a 155-lb person walking briskly: approximately 12,500 steps.

  • Yes. At 2.0 mph you burn about 30-35 calories per 1,000 steps. At 3.0 mph brisk it's 38-45. At 4.0 mph fast walking it's 55-65 calories per 1,000 steps. The difference between slow and fast walking is roughly 70-80% more calorie burn.

  • Calorie burn is directly proportional to body weight because heavier bodies require more energy to move. A 220-lb person burns 43% more calories walking the same route as a 154-lb person.

  • MET-based calculations are accurate to within 15-20% for most people. Accuracy improves when you use your measured step length and actual walking pace rather than estimates.

Methodology

This calculator uses the MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) method from the Ainsworth et al. 2011 Compendium of Physical Activities.

Calories = MET x weight (kg) x time (hours)

Time is derived from distance (steps x step length) divided by walking speed. MET values range from 2.0 (slow walk) to 6.3 (power walk at 4.5 mph). Default step lengths are based on published gait research. Results represent gross caloric expenditure; net calories above rest are approximately 75-80% of displayed values.

10,000 steps burns approximately 300-500 calories for most adults, depending on body weight and walking pace. A 155-lb person walking briskly burns about 400 calories per 10,000 steps and covers roughly 4.7 miles. The MET-based formula used is: Calories = MET x weight(kg) x time(hours). At a brisk 3.0 mph pace (MET 3.5), each 1,000 steps burns roughly 40 calories for a 155-lb person. Heavier individuals burn more; lighter individuals burn less. Walking speed significantly affects calorie burn per step.
