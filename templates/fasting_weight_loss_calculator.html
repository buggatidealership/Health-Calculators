What Is Intermittent Fasting Weight Loss?

Intermittent fasting (IF) promotes weight loss by creating a caloric deficit through time-restricted eating windows. During fasting periods, your body depletes glycogen stores and shifts to burning stored fat for energy. This calculator estimates fat loss and water weight changes based on your BMR, activity level, and chosen fasting protocol using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation.

How to Use the Fasting Weight Loss Calculator

This calculator estimates how much fat you may lose while following intermittent fasting protocols based on your body composition and fasting frequency.
  • Weight: Used to calculate your baseline fat mass and energy reserves.
  • Height: Helps calculate your starting BMI.
  • Age: Impacts basal metabolic rate (BMR).
  • Gender: Affects body fat percentage and metabolic rate.
  • Fasting Type: Choose between popular protocols like 16:8, 18:6, or 20:4 to model expected loss.

Estimates are based on clinical fasting research and average fat loss rates over time. Weekly changes are modeled by fasting intensity and consistency.

Activity Level Guide

Activity factors range from 1.2 (sedentary) to 1.9 (extra active) and account for all movement beyond basal metabolism, including exercise, walking, fidgeting, and occupational activity. Most desk workers overestimate their activity level — if unsure, choose one level lower than you think.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to lose weight with intermittent fasting?

Most people see initial water weight loss within 24-48 hours of starting intermittent fasting. More significant fat loss typically begins after 2-3 weeks of consistent fasting, with visible results often appearing after 4-8 weeks. Consistency is key for long-term results.

Will I lose muscle during intermittent fasting?

Some muscle loss is possible during extended fasting periods, but it's minimal when fasting is done correctly. To preserve muscle mass, maintain adequate protein intake during eating windows (1.6-2.2g per kg of body weight) and incorporate resistance training into your routine 2-3 times per week.

Which intermittent fasting schedule is best for weight loss?

The most effective schedule is one you can maintain consistently. Many find success with 16:8 fasting (16 hours fasting, 8 hours eating) as it's sustainable. For more aggressive results, some prefer 20:4 or alternate day fasting, though these may be harder to maintain long-term.

Can I exercise while fasting?

Yes, exercise during fasting can enhance fat burning. Low to moderate intensity activities like walking, yoga, or light resistance training are well-tolerated during fasted states. For high-intensity training, consider scheduling workouts near the end of your fasting period or during your eating window for optimal performance.

Is intermittent fasting safe for everyone?

Intermittent fasting is not recommended for pregnant or breastfeeding women, people with eating disorders, type 1 diabetics, or those with certain medical conditions. Children, adolescents, and elderly individuals should approach fasting cautiously. Always consult a healthcare provider before starting any fasting regimen.

Methodology

This calculator estimates weight loss during intermittent fasting by first calculating Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation: Males: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) + 5; Females: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) - 161. Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) is derived by multiplying BMR by a standard activity factor (1.2-1.9). For daily intermittent fasting protocols (16:8, 18:6, 20:4), the caloric deficit is estimated based on reduced caloric intake during the shortened eating window, assuming approximately 70% of normal hourly calories are consumed during eating hours. For extended fasts (24+ hours), the full TDEE is applied as the deficit. Fat loss is calculated using the standard approximation of 3,500 calories per pound (7,700 kcal per kg) of body fat.

Water weight loss from glycogen depletion is estimated separately (up to 0.5 kg for the first 24 hours, with diminishing additional loss over days 2-3). These are simplified estimates; actual results depend on dietary compliance during eating windows, metabolic adaptation, body composition, and individual metabolic rate variation.
