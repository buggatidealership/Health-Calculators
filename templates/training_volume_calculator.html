How This Calculator Works

This calculator uses evidence-based volume landmarks from peer-reviewed research to generate personalized recommendations. Here's what drives each number:

Base Volume (from Israetel & Schoenfeld research)

Each muscle group has a research-backed volume range defined by three landmarks:

  • MEV (Minimum Effective Volume) — the fewest sets per week that still produce measurable growth
  • MAV (Maximum Adaptive Volume) — the "sweet spot" where most growth occurs relative to effort
  • MRV (Maximum Recoverable Volume) — the ceiling above which recovery fails and progress reverses

Your recommended sets target the midpoint of MAV, adjusted for your inputs.

Recovery Modifiers

Base volume is scaled by three recovery factors:

  • Age: Recovery capacity declines with age due to reduced satellite cell availability and slower muscle protein synthesis. Volume is scaled from 100% (18–30) down to 70% (60+), based on Angleri et al. (2024).
  • Sleep: Sleep deprivation reduces muscle protein synthesis by ~18% and elevates cortisol by ~21% (Lamon et al., 2021). Fair sleep applies a 0.85x modifier; poor sleep applies 0.70x.
  • Stress: Chronic stress elevates cortisol, blunting hypertrophic adaptation. High stress applies a 0.80x modifier.

Per-Session Distribution

Research shows diminishing returns beyond ~10 hard sets per muscle per session (Schoenfeld et al., 2016). Weekly volume is distributed across your training days with a cap of 10 sets per muscle per session. If your target volume exceeds what your frequency can handle, the calculator recommends increasing frequency.

What Counts as a "Set"

A "set" means a working set taken to RPE 7+ (1–3 reps in reserve). Warm-up sets, sets with less than 40% 1RM, and sets stopped well short of failure do not count toward these targets.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many sets per muscle group per week should I do?

Research consistently shows that 10–20 hard sets per muscle group per week is the optimal range for most people. Beginners grow well with 8–12 sets, while advanced lifters may need 16–22 sets. A 2017 meta-analysis by Schoenfeld found a graded dose-response relationship — more sets produce more growth up to roughly 20 sets per week, after which diminishing returns and recovery issues set in. The exact number depends on your experience level, recovery capacity, and which muscle group you're training.

Does training frequency matter if weekly volume is the same?

Frequency is primarily a tool for distributing volume, not an independent growth driver. A 2018 review by Schoenfeld and Grgic found no significant hypertrophy difference between training a muscle 2x vs 3x per week when total weekly sets were equal. The practical rule: train each muscle at least twice per week, and cap sessions at roughly 10 hard sets per muscle. If your weekly target is 16 sets, do 8 sets across 2 sessions rather than 16 in one session.

What counts as a "hard set" for training volume?

A hard set is a working set performed at RPE 7 or higher — meaning you stop with roughly 1 to 3 reps left in the tank (or to failure). Warm-up sets, feeder sets, and sets stopped well short of failure do not count. Sets with less than approximately 40% of your one-rep max are too light to count regardless of effort. The research on volume-hypertrophy relationships specifically measured sets taken close to failure.

Should I train differently as I get older?

Yes, but not as drastically as many people think. Adults lose 3–5% of muscle mass per decade after 30 without resistance training, and recovery capacity does decline with age. Research by Angleri et al. (2024) shows that older adults can still build muscle effectively — they may just need slightly fewer total sets and more recovery time between sessions. The key adjustment is reducing volume by 10–30% compared to younger lifters and allowing more rest days, while maintaining training intensity (how hard each set is).

How does sleep affect training volume?

Significantly. A study by Lamon et al. (2021) found that acute sleep deprivation reduced muscle protein synthesis by 18%, increased cortisol by 21%, and decreased testosterone by 24%. If you consistently sleep under 6 hours, you cannot recover from the same training volume as someone sleeping 7–9 hours. The practical recommendation is to reduce weekly volume by 15–30% during periods of poor sleep rather than training through it — the sets you can't recover from aren't helping you grow.
