What Is HCG Reconstitution?

Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (HCG) is supplied as a lyophilized (freeze-dried) powder that must be mixed with bacteriostatic water before injection. This mixing process is called reconstitution. The amount of water you add determines the concentration of the solution, which in turn determines how much liquid you draw into the syringe for each dose. Getting the math right is essential for accurate dosing.

HCG is commonly prescribed for fertility treatments, testosterone replacement therapy (TRT) support, and other medical uses. Because the powder must be mixed before use, patients often need to calculate their own injection volumes — a step where dosing errors can easily occur. This calculator does the reconstitution math for you, converting your prescribed dose in IU to a measurable volume in mL and insulin syringe units.

How to Reconstitute HCG

Reconstitution is the process of dissolving the freeze-dried HCG powder with bacteriostatic water. Follow these steps carefully:

Step-by-Step Mixing Guide

  • Step 1 — Gather supplies: You will need the HCG vial (lyophilized powder), a vial of bacteriostatic water, alcohol swabs, a mixing syringe (typically 3 mL with an 18-21 gauge needle), and your injection syringes (U-100 insulin syringes, 29-31 gauge).
  • Step 2 — Clean the vial tops: Wipe the rubber stoppers of both the HCG vial and the bacteriostatic water vial with alcohol swabs. Allow to air dry.
  • Step 3 — Draw the water: Using the mixing syringe, draw the desired amount of bacteriostatic water. The amount you add determines your concentration — common choices are 1 mL, 2 mL, or 5 mL.
  • Step 4 — Add water to HCG vial: Insert the needle into the HCG vial and slowly inject the water down the inside wall of the vial. Do not squirt the water directly onto the powder, as this can damage the peptide.
  • Step 5 — Gently mix: Roll the vial gently between your palms for 30-60 seconds. Do not shake the vial — shaking can denature the HCG and reduce its potency.
  • Step 6 — Verify dissolution: The solution should be clear and colorless. If particles remain, continue gently rolling. Do not use the solution if it appears cloudy or discolored.
  • Step 7 — Store properly: Refrigerate the reconstituted HCG at 2-8°C (36-46°F). Label the vial with the date and concentration. Use within 60 days of mixing (or per your pharmacy's guidance).

The amount of bacteriostatic water you add is flexible — it does not change the total IU in the vial, only the concentration. Adding more water means a larger injection volume per dose, which can be easier to measure accurately. Adding less water means a smaller, more concentrated injection.

Methodology

This calculator uses basic reconstitution pharmacology: Concentration (IU/mL) = Vial Strength (IU) / Bacteriostatic Water Volume (mL), and Injection Volume (mL) = Desired Dose (IU) / Concentration (IU/mL). Syringe units are calculated by multiplying the injection volume in mL by 100, based on the standard U-100 insulin syringe where 100 graduated units equal 1 mL. Doses per vial are determined by dividing the total vial strength by the desired dose per injection. These are standard pharmaceutical reconstitution formulas used in clinical practice.

This calculator assumes the use of U-100 insulin syringes and standard HCG lyophilized powder vials reconstituted with bacteriostatic water. Actual reconstitution should follow the specific instructions provided by your prescribing physician or compounding pharmacy. Slight volume losses from dead space in needles and syringes are not accounted for.

Understanding Syringe Units

Most HCG injections are administered using U-100 insulin syringes. Understanding the markings on these syringes is critical for accurate dosing.

U-100 Insulin Syringe Basics

  • 100 units = 1 mL: A U-100 insulin syringe has 100 graduation marks per 1 mL. Each "unit" on the syringe equals 0.01 mL.
  • Common syringe sizes: Insulin syringes come in 0.3 mL (30 units), 0.5 mL (50 units), and 1 mL (100 units). Choose the smallest syringe that fits your injection volume for the most accurate measurement.
  • Syringe units are not IU: The "units" marked on an insulin syringe refer to volume (0.01 mL increments), not International Units of HCG. The two are only equal when the concentration is exactly 100 IU/mL, which is uncommon for HCG.
  • Reading the syringe: Hold the syringe at eye level. Read the top of the plunger (the flat edge closest to the needle). The liquid level should align with the desired graduation mark.

For very small doses (under 5 units on the syringe), accuracy becomes difficult. In these cases, consider adding more bacteriostatic water during reconstitution to increase the injection volume and make measurement easier.

Storage Guidelines

Proper storage is essential to maintain HCG potency after reconstitution.

Storage Requirements

  • Before reconstitution: Unreconstituted HCG powder can be stored at room temperature (up to 25°C / 77°F) until the expiration date on the vial. Keep away from direct light.
  • After reconstitution: Refrigerate immediately at 2-8°C (36-46°F). Do not freeze. Reconstituted HCG is typically stable for 60 days when stored properly, though some pharmacies recommend using within 30 days.
  • Bacteriostatic water: The bacteriostatic agent (benzyl alcohol 0.9%) in the water inhibits bacterial growth, which is why reconstituted HCG lasts longer than if mixed with sterile water. Do not substitute sterile water for bacteriostatic water for multi-dose vials.
  • Signs of degradation: Discard the solution if it becomes cloudy, changes color, or contains visible particles. These indicate the HCG may have degraded or become contaminated.
  • Travel: Keep reconstituted HCG in a cooler with ice packs during travel. Avoid exposing the vial to temperatures above 25°C (77°F) for extended periods.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much bacteriostatic water should I add to my HCG vial?

The amount of bacteriostatic water is flexible and does not change the total IU in the vial — it only changes the concentration. Common choices are 1 mL, 2 mL, or 5 mL. Adding more water results in a larger injection volume per dose, which can be easier to measure accurately with an insulin syringe. For a 5,000 IU vial, adding 2 mL is a popular choice because it yields clean numbers: 250 IU = 0.10 mL (10 units). Follow your prescribing doctor's or pharmacy's specific instructions if provided.

Can I use regular sterile water instead of bacteriostatic water?

For multi-dose vials (where you draw from the vial multiple times), bacteriostatic water is strongly recommended. It contains 0.9% benzyl alcohol, which inhibits bacterial growth and keeps the solution safe for repeated needle punctures over weeks. Regular sterile water lacks this preservative, meaning the vial should be used within 24 hours of reconstitution to avoid contamination risk. If your HCG is a single-use vial, sterile water is acceptable.

What type of syringe should I use for HCG injections?

Most patients use U-100 insulin syringes with 29-31 gauge needles for subcutaneous HCG injections. These syringes have fine graduation marks that allow precise measurement of small volumes. Choose the smallest syringe size that fits your injection volume: a 0.3 mL (30-unit) syringe for doses under 0.3 mL, a 0.5 mL (50-unit) syringe for doses under 0.5 mL, or a 1 mL (100-unit) syringe for larger volumes. Smaller syringes provide more precise measurements.

How long does reconstituted HCG last?

When mixed with bacteriostatic water and stored in the refrigerator at 2-8°C (36-46°F), reconstituted HCG typically remains potent for up to 60 days. Some compounding pharmacies recommend using it within 30 days for maximum effectiveness. Never freeze reconstituted HCG, and discard any solution that appears cloudy or discolored. Always check the specific storage instructions provided by your pharmacy.

Why does my doctor prescribe HCG in IU but my syringe shows units?

This is the most common source of confusion with HCG dosing. Your doctor prescribes a dose in International Units (IU), which measures the biological activity of the HCG. Your insulin syringe displays "units" that measure volume — each unit on a U-100 syringe equals 0.01 mL. The two are completely different measurements. To convert from IU to syringe units, you need to know your solution's concentration (IU per mL), which depends on how much water you added during reconstitution. This is exactly what our calculator computes.
