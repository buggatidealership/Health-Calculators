About the Running Calorie Formula

  • Formula: Calories = MET × Weight (kg) × Duration (hours)
  • MET values: From the Ainsworth et al. (2011) Compendium of Physical Activities — the gold-standard reference for exercise energy expenditure.
  • Gross vs net: Gross calories include resting metabolism; net calories subtract what you'd burn just sitting still (MET 1.0 × kg × hours).
  • Terrain: Uphill running increases calorie burn proportionally to grade using ACSM metabolic equations. Trail adds ~12% for uneven surface resistance.

How Many Calories Does Running Burn?

Running burns approximately 80–140 calories per mile depending on body weight and pace. A 155-pound (70 kg) person burns roughly 100 calories per mile at a 10-minute/mile pace, or about 600 calories during a 60-minute run. Heavier runners burn more; faster runners burn more per minute but similar amounts per mile.

The number of calories burned running depends primarily on three factors: your body weight, the distance covered, and your pace. Heavier runners do more mechanical work with each stride, burning more calories. Faster running increases intensity (higher MET) and burns more calories per minute — but the calorie-per-mile advantage is smaller than most people assume.

How Running Pace Affects Calorie Burn

Faster running is more intense — it uses more oxygen and burns more calories per minute. However, calorie burn per mile stays relatively constant regardless of pace, because you cover the same distance. The main advantage of running faster is finishing your workout sooner, not burning more calories per mile.

Key Insight: Calories Per Mile vs Per Minute

Notice that calories per mile (right column) stays relatively consistent across paces — roughly 100–116 kcal/mile for a 155-lb runner. This is because running is primarily a distance-dependent activity: you do approximately the same mechanical work per mile regardless of speed. Running faster simply completes that work in less time.

The practical takeaway: to burn more calories running, run farther. Running faster burns more calories per minute but not dramatically more per mile.

Methodology

This calculator uses the MET (Metabolic Equivalent of Task) method to estimate calorie expenditure from running. The formula is: Calories = MET × Weight (kg) × Duration (hours). MET values are drawn from the Ainsworth et al. (2011) Compendium of Physical Activities — the gold-standard reference used by exercise physiologists and published in the journal Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise.

Running pace is converted to miles per hour, and the corresponding MET is interpolated from the Compendium table. Terrain adjustments use ACSM (American College of Sports Medicine) metabolic equations for uphill walking/running, which estimate approximately 0.9 mL/kg/min additional VO₂ per percent grade per meter/second of speed.

Two calorie outputs are provided: gross calories (total energy expenditure including resting metabolism) and net calories (energy above what you'd expend at rest). Fitness trackers typically report gross calories; food-logging apps often compare against net calories. The difference is approximately 1 MET × weight_kg × hours.

Accuracy: MET-based formulas are accurate to within ±15% for most runners (Jetté et al., 1990). Actual values vary with individual running economy, terrain, altitude, temperature, and fitness level. This calculator does not account for post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC), which adds approximately 6–15% to total calorie burn for high-intensity runs.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many calories does running burn per mile?

Running burns approximately 80–140 calories per mile, depending on your body weight. A useful rule of thumb: multiply your weight in pounds by 0.63 for an estimate of net calories per mile, or 0.72 for gross calories. For example, a 155-lb runner burns roughly 98–112 calories per mile on flat terrain. Heavier runners burn more per mile because they do more mechanical work against gravity with each stride.

Does running speed affect how many calories I burn?

Running faster burns more calories per minute but only marginally more calories per mile. A 155-lb runner burns about 114 kcal/mile at 6 mph (10:00 pace) versus 102 kcal/mile at 10 mph (6:00 pace) — a difference of only ~12%. The main practical difference: faster running finishes the same distance in less time, so it's a more time-efficient workout. For maximizing total calorie burn, focus on running farther rather than faster.

Do you burn more calories running uphill?

Yes — significantly more. Running up a 5% grade increases calorie burn by approximately 18%, and an 8% grade increases it by about 30%, compared to flat running at the same pace. This is because uphill running requires additional work against gravity. Trail running adds roughly 12% due to uneven surface resistance and the additional stabilizing muscle engagement required. Use the terrain selector in the calculator above to account for this.

What is the difference between gross and net calories burned running?

Gross calories represent your total energy expenditure during the run, including the calories you would have burned just resting. Net calories represent only the additional energy above your resting metabolic rate — the calories "caused by" the exercise itself. For a 155-lb person running 30 minutes, gross calories might be 290 kcal while net calories are 270 kcal (with ~20 kcal subtracted for resting metabolism). Most fitness trackers report gross calories.

How accurate are running calorie calculators?

MET-based running calorie calculators are accurate within approximately ±10–15% for most runners (Jetté et al., 1990). The main sources of error are individual running economy (more efficient runners burn fewer calories at the same pace), exact terrain and grade, and post-exercise oxygen consumption (EPOC). Wrist-based fitness trackers are often less accurate (±20–30%) due to the difficulty of measuring heart rate precisely during vigorous exercise. For the most accurate measurement, a chest-strap heart rate monitor combined with VO₂ data provides the best results.

How many calories does a 30-minute run burn?

A 30-minute run burns approximately 240–400 calories for most people, depending on weight and pace. A 155-lb runner at a 10-minute/mile pace covers 3 miles and burns roughly 295 calories gross. At a faster 8-minute/mile pace (3.75 miles in 30 min), the same runner burns about 414 calories. At a slow 12-minute/mile jog (2.5 miles), they burn about 245 calories.

Is running an efficient way to burn calories?

Running is one of the most calorie-efficient exercises available — it burns 9–14 calories per minute for most runners, compared to 4–6 for walking and 7–11 for cycling. Running's high MET value (8–14 depending on pace) means it burns significantly more calories per unit of time than most other activities. Additionally, high-intensity running produces EPOC (excess post-exercise oxygen consumption), elevating your metabolic rate for 1–6 hours after the run and adding 6–15% to total calorie expenditure.
