What Are Macronutrients?

Macronutrients are the three main nutrients your body needs in large amounts: protein (4 cal/g), carbohydrates (4 cal/g), and fat (9 cal/g). This calculator determines your daily caloric needs using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, then distributes calories across macros based on your goal and diet preference. The split is guided by recommendations from the Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics and ISSN.

How the Calculator Works

This calculator first estimates your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) using the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, then multiplies by an activity factor to get your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE). It adjusts calories for your goal (deficit or surplus), then distributes those calories across protein, carbohydrates, and fat based on your selected diet preference.

Step 1: BMR (Mifflin-St Jeor Equation)

Males: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) + 5

Females: BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) - 161

TDEE = BMR x Activity Factor

Target Calories = TDEE + Goal Adjustment

Step 2: Macronutrient Distribution

Once your target calories are determined, macros are distributed based on your diet preference. The calculator uses the following energy densities:

  • Protein: 4 calories per gram
  • Carbohydrates: 4 calories per gram
  • Fat: 9 calories per gram

The Mifflin-St Jeor equation (1990) replaced the older Harris-Benedict equation because it better reflects modern body compositions. A 2005 ADA validation study found it predicts BMR within 10% for 82% of individuals.

Macronutrient Split Guide

Macronutrient ratios should align with your goal, activity level, and dietary preference. There is no single "best" split — adherence and total calorie intake matter more than exact ratios. The splits below are based on guidelines from the ISSN and Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics.

Frequently Asked Questions

How accurate is this calorie and macro calculator?

This calculator uses the Mifflin-St Jeor equation, which the American Dietetic Association recommends as the most accurate predictive formula. It predicts BMR within 10% for approximately 82% of individuals. However, individual factors like genetics, hormones, body composition, and metabolic conditions can affect your actual needs. Use results as a starting point and adjust based on real-world progress over 2-4 weeks.

What are macronutrients and why do they matter?

Macronutrients are the three main nutrients your body needs in large amounts: protein (4 calories per gram), carbohydrates (4 calories per gram), and fat (9 calories per gram). Each plays a distinct role — protein builds and repairs tissue, carbohydrates fuel activity and brain function, and fat supports hormone production and nutrient absorption. The ratio you eat them in affects body composition, energy levels, and workout performance.

Should I adjust my macros on rest days?

Some people benefit from reducing carbohydrate intake on rest days while maintaining protein to support recovery. A common approach is to eat 10-20% fewer calories on rest days, primarily from carbohydrates, while keeping protein consistent at 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight. However, for most recreational exercisers, keeping macros consistent day-to-day is simpler and equally effective.

How often should I recalculate my calorie and macro needs?

Recalculate after any significant body weight change (5+ pounds or 2+ kg), when your activity level changes substantially, or every 4-6 weeks during active dieting. As you lose weight, your TDEE decreases due to metabolic adaptation. If you are not seeing progress toward your goals after 3-4 weeks of consistent adherence, recalculate and verify your tracking accuracy.

What is the best macro split for weight loss?

For weight loss, research from the International Society of Sports Nutrition (ISSN) recommends higher protein intake (25-35% of calories, or 1.6-2.2 g/kg body weight) to preserve muscle mass during a calorie deficit. A balanced split of 30% protein, 40% carbohydrates, and 30% fat works well for most people. Low-carb approaches can also be effective, but adherence matters more than the exact ratio.

How much protein do I need per day?

The ISSN recommends 1.4-2.0 g of protein per kg of body weight per day for physically active individuals. For those in a calorie deficit trying to preserve muscle, the upper end (1.6-2.2 g/kg) is recommended. Sedentary adults need at least 0.8 g/kg per day (the RDA minimum). This calculator sets protein based on your goal and activity level within these evidence-based ranges.
