Frequently Asked Questions

  • According to the American Diabetes Association, a normal A1C is below 5.7%. An A1C between 5.7% and 6.4% indicates prediabetes, and 6.5% or higher on two separate tests indicates diabetes.

  • The ADA recommends A1C testing at least twice a year for people with well-controlled diabetes, and every 3 months if treatment has recently changed or blood sugar goals are not being met.

  • Yes. A1C results can be affected by hemoglobin variants (HbS, HbC, HbE), iron deficiency anemia, recent blood transfusions, and kidney disease. In these cases, your doctor may use fructosamine testing or CGM as alternatives.

  • Estimated Average Glucose (eAG) is derived from your A1C and represents your average blood sugar over 2-3 months. Fasting glucose captures a single moment. The ADA formula is: eAG (mg/dL) = 28.7 x A1C - 46.7.

  • Meaningful changes typically take 2-3 months. A drop of 0.5-1.0% per quarter is considered good progress with lifestyle modifications alone.

Methodology

This calculator uses the ADAG formula endorsed by the ADA:

eAG (mg/dL) = 28.7 × A1C − 46.7

The reverse conversion uses: A1C = (eAG + 46.7) / 28.7. Unit conversion between mg/dL and mmol/L uses the standard factor of 18.0. Risk categories follow ADA diagnostic thresholds: normal (<5.7%), prediabetes (5.7-6.4%), and diabetes (≥6.5%).

A1C (HbA1c) measures average blood sugar over 2-3 months. The ADA formula converts A1C to estimated average glucose: eAG (mg/dL) = 28.7 x A1C - 46.7. A normal A1C is below 5.7%, prediabetes is 5.7-6.4%, and diabetes is 6.5% or higher. For example, an A1C of 7.0% corresponds to an average blood sugar of about 154 mg/dL. The test is the primary tool for diagnosing and monitoring diabetes management.
